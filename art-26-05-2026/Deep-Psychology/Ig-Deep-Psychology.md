**Slide 2 — Social Comparison**
**Alt Text:** A seesaw graphic showing one person rising while another falls.
1️⃣ Social Comparison Theory
When peers see you claiming authority, it forces upward comparison. A 2025 study in the *Journal of Digital Sociology* found this drops peer engagement by 41%. 

**Slide 3 — Imposter Syndrome**
**Alt Text:** A person looking in a mirror, but the reflection is a small, worried figure. 
2️⃣ Professional Imposter Syndrome
*The Cyberpsychology Review (2026)* found 72% of passive users suffer from "self-focused attention bias." They fear engaging with smart content exposes their own perceived inadequacies.

**Slide 4 — FNE**
**Alt Text:** A person typing a comment on a phone, surrounded by floating, judgmental eyes.
3️⃣ Fear of Negative Evaluation (FNE)
Users often delete comments because of FNE. *Behavioral Science in Tech (2025)* proves we suppress engagement out of fear that mutual connections will judge our opinions.

**Slide 5 — Dual-Role Trust**
**Alt Text:** A split screen showing a friendly handshake with a stranger and a competitive race with a peer.
4️⃣ The Dual-Role Trust Model
According to *Trust in Digital Networks Quarterly (2026)*, we trust strangers for "inspiration" but view our immediate peers through a lens of subconscious "competition."

**Slide 6 — Interest Graph Shift**
**Alt Text:** A network node diagram morphing into a targeted dartboard.
5️⃣ The Interest Graph Shift
We have moved from the Social Graph (who you know) to the Interest Graph (what you like). *Social Algorithm Studies (2025)* shows stranger engagement is now functionally superior.

**Slide 7 — Status Transaction**
**Alt Text:** A Like button depicted as a shiny gold coin being handed over. 
6️⃣ Status Transaction Theory
The *Stanford Online Behavior Lab (2026)* proved every "Like" is a micro-transaction of social status. We guard this currency among close ties but give it freely to distant strangers.
