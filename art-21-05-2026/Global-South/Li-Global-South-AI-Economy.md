## The AI Divide: Why the Global South Can't Afford to Be Left Behind

When we talk about the economic impact of Artificial Intelligence, the numbers are staggering. Projections suggest AI could inject over $15 trillion into the global economy by 2030. But dig a little deeper into where that value is accumulating, and a stark reality emerges. 

According to the **UNCTAD Technology and Innovation Report 2025**, the lion's share of this unprecedented growth is concentrated in the Global North—specifically the United States and China. For countries in the Global South, the AI revolution presents a complex dual challenge: the promise of immense developmental leaps shadowed by the existential threat of a widening technological divide.

The barriers to entry are primarily infrastructural and geopolitical. To compete in the AI economy, nations need massive "compute" power—data centers, stable energy grids, and advanced connectivity. Currently, the Global South relies heavily on North-centric infrastructure, creating a dangerous dependency that stifles local innovation. Furthermore, with the majority of private R&D controlled by a handful of mega-corporations, emerging economies are often relegated to the role of passive consumers rather than active creators.

But it’s not all bleak. We are seeing incredible resilience through grassroots innovation. From building language-first models tailored to African dialects to deploying robust digital public infrastructure in India and Latin America, local ecosystems are fighting to "decolonize" AI. 

To turn the tide, global policy must shift. We need targeted investments in digital literacy to transition workers into AI-augmented roles, and we must ensure equitable representation in international AI governance forums. The Global South must not merely adopt foreign tools; it must have the sovereignty to build AI that solves its own unique challenges.
